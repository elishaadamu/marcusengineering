Marcus Engineering's heritage in the aerospace industry is founded on in-depth experience with both civil and military flight applications, encompassing both traditional and unmanned aircraft systems. Collaborating closely with our clients, we delve deep into understanding the nuances of your product, ensuring it aligns with relevant Technical Standard Orders (TSOs), and rigorously preparing a validation and certification package that stands up to scrutiny.

Whether you're aiming to develop an aircraft subsystem that aligns with a manufacturer's standards or need to secure a Technical Standard Order (TSO) Authorization, we stand ready to assist. Recognizing the wide range of possible failure outcomes, from minor to catastrophic, we offer comprehensive guidance through design and certification processes, catering to systems across the spectrum from DAL A through to DAL D.

Developing for the aerospace sector demands a keen understanding of a multitude of standards and requirements. We are adept at navigating industry norms such as ARP4754A (Guidelines for Development of Civil Aircraft and Systems) and DO-178C (Software Considerations in Airborne Systems and Equipment Certification), as well as their guidance and application documentation. These include FAA AC (Advisory Circular) 20-174's Development of Civil Aircraft and Systems, and FAA AC 20-115C's Airborne Software Assurance. In the aerospace realm, controlling documents often mirror military versions of civil requirements. Examples include the RTCA DO-160 vis-à-vis MIL-STD-810 and MIL-STD-704. These controlling documents might also take the form of TSO standards from the FAA or ETSOs from EASA, underscoring the minimum performance standards for specific materials and parts utilized in civil aircraft.

Employing our trademark R2E™ (Risk Reduction Engineering™) Design Controls, along with our sophisticated Hardware and Software development processes, we ensure products meet functional, performance, and customer requirements. However, compliance isn't the sole focus. It's paramount to maintain documented traceability throughout the development lifecycle — from initial requirements, navigating hazards, through to specifications, implementation, verification, and validation.
